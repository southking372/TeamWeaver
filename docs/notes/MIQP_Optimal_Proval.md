In our framework, we address this challenge through a dual-layered approach: a mathematically rigorous solving strategy for the instantaneous assignment (MIQP), and a closed-loop self-recovery mechanism governed by the ReAct paradigm during dynamic execution.

\textbf{(1) Mathematical Guarantee of Global Optimality in Task Assignment (Why MIQP)}
The primary optimization problem in our framework is formulated as a Mixed-Integer Quadratic Program (MIQP). Unlike non-linear programming (Non-LP) approaches, which rely on gradient descent and are inherently susceptible to local minima, our formulation guarantees global optimality for the instantaneous assignment sub-tasks due to its structural convexity and exact solving strategies.

\textbf{\emph{(i) Convexity of the Objective and Feasible Region:}} As defined in Eq.(1), the objective function consists of continuous convex and linear terms. Specifically, the margin penalty $\lambda_D ||\mathbf{D}||_2^2$ is strictly convex, and the transition cost $\lambda_T ||\mathbf{T}(\mathbf{A} - \mathbf{A_p})||_1$ (an L1 norm) is convex and easily linearized. Furthermore, the constraints (a) through (d) strictly define a convex polytope for the continuous relaxation of the problem. All variables—such as the allocation matrix $\mathbf{A}$, planning policy $\boldsymbol{\pi}$, and relaxation vector $\boldsymbol{\phi}$—operate within tightly bounded subsets (e.g., $\boldsymbol{\phi} \in \{0,1\}^M$ and $\delta_m \ge -\phi_m \delta_{\max}$).

\textbf{\emph{(ii) Exact Solving Strategy:}} Because the continuous relaxation of our problem is strictly convex and bounded, standard exact solvers (such as Gurobi) employ the Branch-and-Bound (B&B) algorithm.  The B&B algorithm systematically explores the discrete solution space and evaluates convex subproblems at each node. This entirely circumvents the concept of "local minima," as the solver is mathematically guaranteed to either converge to the global optimum or rigorously prove infeasibility within a finite optimality gap.

\textbf{(2) System-Level Stability and Trajectory Convergence (ReAct Mechanism)}
While the MIQP guarantees global optimality at discrete decision intervals, the continuous physical execution requires robustness against environmental uncertainties, dynamic trajectory drifts, and spatial deadlocks. That lies in our ReAct execution procedure.

\textbf{\emph{(i) Temporal Stability via Transition Costs:}} To guarantee that the convergence of the MIQP solver translates to stable physical convergence, we explicitly introduce the transition cost term $\lambda_T ||\mathbf{T}(\mathbf{A} - \mathbf{A_p})||_1$. By penalizing unnecessary deviations from the previous allocation $\mathbf{A_p}$, we mathematically prevent assignment oscillation (chattering). This ensures that the global sequence of optimal allocations converges smoothly over time.

\textbf{\emph{(ii) Self-Recovery via the ReAct Paradigm:}} The dynamic execution is embedded within a ReAct (Reasoning and Acting) loop driven by the Llama-3-8B-Instruct model. Crucially, the perception layer in our framework maintains a global view of the environment and the capability-feature model. If a robot experiences kinematic after-effects or encounters physical constraints that halt its progress, the global state is updated and a Replan is triggered.

\textbf{\emph{(iii) Robustness to After-effects:}} Because the perception is global rather than local, any spatial trajectory deviations do not trap the system in spatial local minima. Instead, the task requirement relaxation ($\boldsymbol{\phi}$) and load penalties ($\delta_m$) absorb the temporary infeasibility, and the LLM re-evaluates the contextual capability assessment $\mathcal{L}$, our perception-plan-skill framework is capable for this execution (Plan and skill after perception fusion). This self-recovery capability guarantees that the multi-robot system continuously progresses toward the global semantic objective without stalling.

In conclusion, the strict convexity of our MIQP formulation guarantees global optimality at the assignment level, while the explicit transition penalties and global perception within the ReAct framework ensure stable, deadlock-free convergence during physical execution.
